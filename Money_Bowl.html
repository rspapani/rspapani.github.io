Ee Sala Cup Namde?¶

A statistical exploration of the Indian Premier League by Rishi Papani¶

0. Introduction and Setup¶

IPL or the Indian Premier League is an Indian mens cricket league and is the most popular cricket league in the world in terms of attendance, and in India in terms of viewership. The series consists of T20 (20 over matches) played between 10 teams from across India. As a refresher, cricket is batting sport where a ball is bowlled towards a batsman who then has to to hit it. The aim of the bowler (person throwing the ball) is to knock over the wicket (wooden rods standing behind the batsman), this scores a wicket which scores out the current batsman. The batsman's objective is to hit the ball with the bat as far as possible before then running back and forth from the wicket mound and the pitcher's mound - at which point the fielding team attempts to strike either him or either mound with the ball also scoring him out. Each time a batsman makes a successive run to either mound they score a point, additionally if they hit the ball far enough they are automatically awarded either 4 or 6 points depending on the distance the ball travels. Lastly, when a team is batting they may only have up to 10 wickets (players scored out) before their turn batting is over, at which point they switch positions with the fielding team. Each game consists of one team batting and then another, each either until they get 10 wickets or they exhaust their maximum allowed balls of 120 (each ball is a pitch essentially from the opposing team's bowler).

One particularly well known team in the IPL is RCB (Royal Challengers Bangalore), who despite having some of the most passionate fans in the league are yet to score a single IPL series win (this is essentially to win the tournament). To make matters even more bizarre, they also have some of the best batsmen in the league (in terms of ability to score runs) as well as in the world (i.e. Virat Kohli is an RCB player who is also the captain of India's national team, and recognized internationally as one of the best batsmen of all time). A common reason that people provide for RCB's predicament is that when players get auctioned on, RCB tends to go for a less balanced team. This means a few players who stand out in the cricket world, along with many players who are well below statistical average in terms of match outcomes. The reasoning goes that as such, RCB is excessively dependant on a few players whose single failures in a match can cause the whole team to lose (i.e. they get out first). Essentially, that although these star players are very good individually, they're not good enough to carry the entire performance of the team.

The purpose of this tutorial is to test our hypothesis is that teams excessively dependant on fewer players tend to have poorer performance thorughout the series as compared to their more balance counterparts. For the purpose of this tutorial, we measure this dependence with regards to how even a team is in terms of player ability - a metric whose calculation we will describe at a later point. Additionally, we also aim to see if RCB is less balanced as a whole when compared to other teams in the league.

1. Data Wrangling¶

First we'll be reading our data (obtained [here]https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/patrickb1912/ipl-complete-dataset-20082020?select=IPL+Matches+2008-2020.csv)) and organizing it into an easily accessible data structure. Our dataset has two csv files, one for broader data regarding every IPL Match between 2008-2020 and another for specific data on each individual ball for each individual match in this period. Firstly, we'll only be considering matches within the past 5 seasons, this is because IPL team strategies can vary greatly by season so a large aggregate would dull many significant statistical trends. This means that elements of a team that would work at one point wouldn't work at other points (i.e. it may have been better in the past to be more balanced, as compared to now).

2. Computing Player Ability¶

Firstly, we'll be analyzing the per team, per game effects of being unbalanced. To measure balance we'll be taking the percentage contribution of the total runs in a game of each player. Here a more unbalanced and singularly dependant team would have less players taking a much larger share of the runs. To measure variation on this, essentially balance, we'll observe the distribution of this metric. We'll do this by calculating the standard deviation of each player's contribution to each game per team.

First we'll calculate individual contribution for batsmen with the above metric of contribution to runs.

An analysis of the above¶

From the chart we can see two clear skews, and really evidence of two separate datasets. Firstly, that of when team 1 wins, shown by the red data points. Here the points cluster to the center but there is significant spread to where the team one standard deviation of contribution is greater than that of the team 2 standard deviation of contribution. Here we can see in this area that the vast majority of points are red. This means that greater deviation here often leads to a more successful result for the team, in other words more deviation and less balance is better. The same can be seen mirrored in where team 2 wins (blue data points), here a greater deviation of team 2 seems to lead to team 2 being more likely to win (as evidenced by a greater number of blue data points in that area). Something else interesting to note is that draws only occur when both teams have a similar and low standard deviation, in other words the teams are more balanced.

4. Conclusion¶

From the above two charts we can observe two things. Firstly, on our scatter plot, whenever a team has a significantly larger standard deviation of run contribution than the other, they are much more likely to win. Secondly, that Royal Challengers Bangalore doesn't have a significantly larger average standard distribution of run contribution as compared to other teams. In fact there are many other teams that sport a higher average standard deviation of run contribution per match, and also are more spread out on the higher end than RCB. In terms of our hypothesis, we were wrong, not only does better balance lead to worse outcomes per match but RCB is also not any more significantly unbalanced than other teams. Although this could just be a limitation or mirage of our methods, for the time being it looks like the common argument is just wrong by these means. And lastly, the title 'ee sala cup namde' is just a local team cheer meaning 'this time the cup is ours', but after trying to diagnose the problem here, it looks like we'll need some more statistical analysis before we can really say that.
